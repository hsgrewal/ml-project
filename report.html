Background

Using devices such as Jawbone Up, Nike FuelBand, and Fitbit it is now possible to collect a large amount of data about personal activity relatively inexpensively. These type of devices are part of the quantified self movement a group of enthusiasts who take measurements about themselves regularly to improve their health, to find patterns in their behavior, or because they are tech geeks. One thing that people regularly do is quantify how much of a particular activity they do, but they rarely quantify how well they do it.

In this project, I will use the data from accelerometers on the belt, forearm, arm, and dumbell of 6 participants. The participants were asked to perform barbell lifts correctly and incorrectly in 5 different ways. More information is available from the website here: http://web.archive.org/web/20161224072740/http:/groupware.les.inf.puc-rio.br/har (see the section on the Weight Lifting Exercise Dataset).

Read the Data

After downloading the data from the data source, I read the data from the two files.

trainRaw <- read.csv("./pml-training.csv")
testRaw <- read.csv("./pml-testing.csv")

Clean the Data

First, I remove the variables containing missing values.

trainData <- trainRaw[, colSums(is.na(trainRaw)) == 0]
testRaw <- testRaw[, colSums(is.na(testRaw)) == 0]

The dimensions of the datasets after removing variables with missing values are shown below.

dim(trainData)
## [1] 19622    93
dim(testRaw)
## [1] 20 60

Next, I remove the first seven variables because they have little impact on classe.

trainData <- trainData[, -c(1:7)]
testRaw <- testRaw[, -c(1:7)]

The dimensions of the datasets after removing low impact variables are shown below.

dim(trainData)
## [1] 19622    86
dim(testRaw)
## [1] 20 53

Lastly, I remove near-zero-variance (NZV) variables from data.

nzv <- nearZeroVar(trainData)
trainData <- trainData[, -nzv]

The dimensions of the datasets after removing near-zero-variance variables are shown below.

dim(trainData)
## [1] 19622    53

Split the Data

Next, I prepare the data for prediction by splitting the training data into a training set and a testing set. The ratio of training to testing data is 70-30.

set.seed(2021)
